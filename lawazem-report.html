Executive summary

Lawazem is a Saudi-based B2B procurement platform founded in 2020, positioning itself as a "one-stop shop" for all office and operational supplies. Its value proposition rests on consolidating many suppliers into one catalog, automating approvals and spend controls, and delivering free next-day service.

The core tension Key finding

Lawazem's item prices include a 10–15% markup (noted as "Cost+" on Medium/Enterprise plans). In contrast, competitors often promise low or guaranteed pricing. On one test, a Dell laptop was SAR 5,799 on Lawazem versus ~SAR 4,526 at another reseller — roughly 28% above market.

  • Prices are generally comparable or slightly above retail for common office items.
  • Jarir Corporate offers a "1 Year Price Guaranteed" — it would match or beat Lawazem on many items.
  • The premium is partially justified by free one-day delivery, enterprise-grade tools, and consolidated invoicing.
  • For large companies eliminating ad-hoc sourcing, the platform efficiency can offset the markup.

Bottom line Verdict

Lawazem offers strong value for large, process-driven buyers (enterprises, government agencies, hospitals) but cost-sensitive or small-scale buyers might prefer competitors with lower item prices. The premium buys fast delivery, corporate-level controls, and ease of management — benefits that must be leveraged to justify the cost.

Services & features

Lawazem markets itself as "the first B2B marketplace built specifically to solve operational purchases and office supplies needs." Its platform is designed for companies, not consumers.

Procurement platform

  • Unified Catalog: Single online catalog spanning hundreds of brands — stationery, cleaning, groceries, IT, AV, and more. Companies can consolidate suppliers and standardize SKUs.
  • Corporate Controls: Branch/department segmentation, cost centers, multi-stage cart approvals, and delegated buying roles — managers can set budgets and control spend.
  • Procurement Automation: Schedule recurring orders, bulk "one-click" ordering for multiple locations, RFQs for special pricing, combined invoices across orders.
  • Analytics & Reports: Built-in spend analytics with tagging and dashboards for company-wide visibility. Executive dashboards enforce policy across branches.

Operations & extras

  • Logistics & Delivery: Free one-day delivery to business locations for all plans. In-app order tracking with one-touch "Delivered" status and returns.
  • Additional Services: Goes beyond supplies — arranges hospitality and gifts (flowers, employee benefits), internal events, meeting prep, and concierge support for international procurement.
  • SmartStock: Inventory management feature for companies with internal stores — manages on-site inventory of parts/supplies in real time.
  • AI-driven catalog: Reportedly uses AI for product suggestions and spend analytics, though independent verification is limited.

Team & funding

The founding team has deep procurement experience — co-founders have 20+ years in procurement/IT/retail. Lawazem's rapid growth (150% quarterly in 2021) and a $1.3M Seed round have been used to refine technology and scale operations. Recognized as Deloitte Fast 50 Rising Star and #1 in MENA.

Pricing structure & plans

Lawazem uses tiered plans tailored to company size and spend. Plans (Small, Medium, Enterprise) are unpriced publicly but defined by typical monthly spend (≥SAR 5k, 10k, 100k) and included seats/storage.

How the markup works

  • Cost+ Markup: Medium-plan customers pay a 15% fee on purchases; Enterprise customers pay 10%. Small-plan companies pay higher base prices with no markup feature.
  • Cashback: Annual cashback of 1–2% on spend partially offsets the markup.
  • No subscription fee: "No additional fee other than the product cost listed" — companies pay only for products (plus the markup).
  • Free delivery: All plans include free one-day shipping to business locations — a premium service that has real cost and value.

Payment terms

  • Flexible billing: Credit card (immediate) or buy-now-pay-later (30-day) options.
  • Corporate PO: 30-day payment terms available for corporate accounts.
  • Consolidated billing: Multiple orders can be combined into a single invoice across branches.
  • Enterprise RFQs: Enterprise customers can request volume pricing on custom orders, but the process is not fully self-serve.

Price reality check High impact

In practice, Lawazem's unit prices tend to be higher than retail. Example findings from the analysis:

  • Dell laptop: SAR 5,799 on Lawazem vs ~SAR 4,526 at another reseller — approximately 28% above market.
  • HP Color LaserJet Pro 4303fdw: ~SAR 2,099 on Lawazem vs SAR 2,299 at Jarir — Lawazem is cheaper here, though Jarir's price guarantee would match it.
  • Lawazem's own FAQ admits pricing scales with company size/spend; the visible Cost+ markup confirms a premium.
  • Lawazem advises clients to consider total cost (including delivery and admin), not just unit price — a fair point for large buyers.

Target segments & use cases

Lawazem's design targets mid-to-large enterprises with recurring bulk supply needs. Co-founder biographies and press explicitly mention large corporations, government agencies, and NGOs as clients.

Best fit — who benefits most Strong fit

  • Office supply management for large businesses (schools, banks, hotel chains) needing branded stationery, printer supplies, breakroom goods, janitorial items.
  • IT/AV procurement where standardization and bulk deals are needed — ordering monitors and conference-room tech across many offices.
  • Inventory control scenarios: companies with internal stores can use SmartStock to manage on-site inventory in real time.
  • Event/hospitality coordination for firms that frequently host guests, since Lawazem offers catering supplies, floral arrangements, and event rentals via partners.
  • Cash-flow-sensitive buyers who need flexible payment — the BNPL terms help smooth budgets.

Poor fit — who shouldn't use it Wrong audience

  • Small businesses and startups with lean operations — Lawazem plans assume thousands of SAR in monthly spend, and multi-approver workflows may not be needed.
  • Price-driven buyers — companies that can negotiate directly with distributors may get better unit prices outside the platform.
  • Industrial / specialized sectors — Lawazem does not cover manufacturing supplies, bulk chemicals, or engineering parts. Companies with broader sourcing needs will still need multiple vendors.
  • Very small businesses (5-person companies) — the 10–15% higher product cost is unlikely to be justified by convenience alone.

Risks & weaknesses

Six structural risks that could limit Lawazem's growth or erode its premium positioning.

Price sensitivity
High risk
Confirmed 10–15% markup on vendor prices. If a client's priority is purely low price, Lawazem can seem expensive. Companies with procurement specialists may prefer haggling or bulk buying elsewhere.
Limited public feedback
Medium risk
Few third-party reviews or case studies. Without robust testimonials or ratings, new prospects must rely on company claims — slowing sales to conservative buyers.
Platform dependency
Medium risk
Clients become dependent on Lawazem's app; any downtime or technical issues could disrupt operations. Traditional suppliers can be contacted directly. If integration with a company's ERP fails, promised savings may not materialize.
Market competition
High risk
The Saudi B2B space is crowded. Jarir has entrenched relationships and supplier bargaining power. Other startups (local and international) may replicate Lawazem's model. Continuous innovation is required to maintain the premium edge.
Specialization limits
Medium risk
Lawazem focuses on "operational procurement" (offices, facilities). It does not cover industrial or specialized sectors. Companies with broader sourcing needs will still need multiple vendors.
Minimum spend requirements
Medium risk
The tiered plan structure requires minimum monthly spend (e.g. SAR 5,000/mo for the "Small" tier). Very small businesses may find the entry barrier too high, limiting the addressable market.

Recommendation

Lawazem offers fair value — but primarily for the clients it was designed for. Its strengths (consolidation, speed, tech) align with the needs of large B2B buyers.

Fair value assessment

For Lawazem's target customers — medium-to-large companies that consume significant office/operational supplies — the platform offers good value despite higher prices. The premium (~10–15%) buys fast delivery, corporate-level control (budgets, approvals), and ease of management. These businesses often incur hidden costs from decentralized buying (missed discounts, lost invoices, emergency courier fees), so Lawazem's efficiencies can more than offset the markup.

A corporate client saving even 2–3% on ordering errors or waste through better analytics may recover more than the platform fee. The Deloitte Fast-50 ranking and venture funding suggest Lawazem is delivering on its promises and scaling successfully.

Pros

  • All-in-one solution: Centralizes all operational purchases (office, groceries, IT) in one platform. Saves management overhead vs multiple vendors.
  • Fast, free delivery: One-day shipping included — avoids multiple courier fees or stockouts.
  • Procurement controls: Built-in workflows (approvals, budgets, spend tracking) reduce maverick spending and enhance compliance.
  • Analytics & insights: Detailed spend reports can reveal savings opportunities, justifying the markup.
  • Additional services: Handles gifts, events, and concierge procurement — unique to Lawazem.
  • Experienced team & growth: Founders with 20+ years procurement experience; ranked #1 on Deloitte Fast 50 MENA.

Cons / Risks

  • Higher unit prices: 10–15% markup is higher than many distributors. Small customers may find cheaper sources outside the platform.
  • Niche focus: Only covers office/facility supplies. Specialized or bulk industrial items not available.
  • Startup risk: Less established brand/trust than legacy suppliers. Few independent reviews or guarantees beyond company claims.
  • Dependence on tech: If the platform or app goes down, internal processes may be disrupted.
  • Minimum scale: Plans assume thousands of SAR/month spend — very small firms may not qualify or see ROI.
  • Competitive market: Big players (Jarir, Amazon) or other B2B startups may push prices down or add features, eroding Lawazem's edge.

Cost drivers that justify the premium

  • Integrated procurement platform: Building and maintaining the SaaS layer (analytics, approvals, mobile apps, accounting integration) incurs significant cost. Clients pay extra to replace ad-hoc sourcing (emails, calls to many suppliers) with an automated system.
  • Fast logistics: Free next-day delivery to many branches requires local warehousing or rapid courier networks. For mission-critical items (printer toner, medical gloves), rapid delivery can justify a markup.
  • Payment/financing: Offering 30-day payment terms effectively extends credit to clients, which requires capital and credit management — some of the markup covers this cost.
  • Quality and reliability: Lawazem partners with verified suppliers. Enterprise buyers often need assurance that low-cost doesn't mean counterfeit.
  • Localization & support: As a Saudi-founded company, Lawazem offers better localization (Arabic interface, Saudi accounting rules, local customer support) than generic global alternatives.

The bottom line

Lawazem's premium pricing is offset by its premium service — but only for clients who leverage its full capabilities. If a buyer values consolidation, speed, and corporate controls and has the budget, Lawazem's higher prices are justified. Companies solely seeking the lowest sticker price should compare with Jarir, wholesalers, or other B2B platforms to verify the platform benefits outweigh the cost difference.
